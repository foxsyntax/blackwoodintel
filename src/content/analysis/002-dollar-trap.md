The conventional narrative about dollar dominance is a story of American power. The United States issues the world's reserve currency; therefore, it can borrow cheaply, run persistent deficits, and project financial influence globally. This narrative is accurate as far as it goes. It does not go very far.

What the narrative omits is the constraint side of the ledger. Reserve currency status is not a free lunch. It is an arrangement with specific structural costs — costs that accumulate quietly until they become impossible to ignore.

## The Triffin Dilemma, Revisited

In 1960, economist Robert Triffin identified a fundamental tension in the Bretton Woods system. For the world to have enough dollars to conduct international trade, the United States had to run persistent current account deficits. But persistent deficits would eventually undermine confidence in the dollar's value. The system contained the seeds of its own instability.

Bretton Woods collapsed in 1971. The Triffin dilemma did not. It simply changed form.

Under the current system — often called Bretton Woods II — the United States continues to supply the world with dollar liquidity by running persistent current account deficits. The surplus countries (China, Germany, Japan, oil exporters) recycle those dollars back into US financial assets, particularly Treasury bonds. This keeps US interest rates lower than they would otherwise be and allows the United States to consume more than it produces.

The arrangement works, up to a point. The point is structural deindustrialization.

## The Manufacturing Transfer

When a country's currency is persistently in demand as a global reserve asset, that currency trades at a premium relative to what pure current account fundamentals would imply. An overvalued currency makes exports expensive and imports cheap. Over decades, this premium hollows out the traded goods sector.

This is not a new observation. It is well-documented in the economics literature. What is less frequently observed is the *political* consequence: the communities most affected by this deindustrialization tend to be precisely those with the least voice in the policy decisions that produced it.

The financial sector benefits from dollar dominance. It does not experience the costs. The manufacturing sector in the American Midwest experiences the costs. It does not capture the benefits. This distributional asymmetry is durable and self-reinforcing.

## What the Data Shows

Between 1980 and 2023, the US current account balance moved from approximate equilibrium to a structural deficit averaging roughly 3% of GDP. Over the same period, manufacturing employment fell from 19.5 million to 12.9 million — not primarily due to automation (which is real but overstated as an explanation) but due to the combination of dollar overvaluation and trade liberalization with countries that did not face equivalent constraints.

The Federal Reserve's ability to conduct independent monetary policy has also been compromised. Rate decisions that would be appropriate for domestic conditions must account for the global dollar system. A rate increase that is correct for the US economy may trigger capital flow reversals that destabilize emerging market economies — and those instabilities loop back into US financial conditions.

## The Instability Accumulation

Reserve currency systems have a life cycle. They do not end through sudden collapse. They end through the gradual accumulation of structural distortions until alternative arrangements become attractive enough to overcome the coordination costs of switching.

That process is underway. It is slow. It is not linear. And it is not yet at a stage where the outcome is determinable.

What is determinable: the costs of dollar dominance are no longer abstract. They are recorded in employment statistics, regional economic data, and the political landscape that those statistics have produced.
